Income and Inequality Across the Old Line State

Per capita income in Maryland increased across all counties from 2010-2020, according to the Maryland department of planning. Each county saw similar trend lines throughout the past decade, with notable spikes across the board in 2020.

Maryland Per Capita Personal Income (Current Dollars): 2010-2020

Maryland is the wealthiest state in the country, according to U.S. News & World Report. Evidently, it has also seen a rise in income inequality, with the richest 5% of households earning 11.7 times as much as the poorest fifth.

From the 1970s to the mid-2000s, the rich got richer at an exponentially faster rate than other Marylanders.

These trends permeate every facet of Maryland life, from school quality to gas prices, and were addressed by now-Gov. Wes Moore on the campaign trail.

After assuming office, Moore championed policies that sought to hasten the statewide minimum wage increase, expand payments to low-income people with jobs and provide incentives for banks to open in low-income neighborhoods.

While the overall per-capita rise in income applied across all counties, the wealthier ones still benefitted at a faster rate. Montgomery County saw the highest increase in dollar amount over the last decade and remains Maryland’s most populous and richest county. Howard County is also one of the wealthiest in the nation.

Across the state, people and their governments are reckoning with the realities of being wealthy on paper, but deeply unequal in practice. The Gini index, which measures inequality in a region on a scale from 0 to 1, registers a .46 for Maryland, making it a state with “high income disparity,” according to UNICEF.

This is actually lower than the nationwide coefficient of .48, highlighting the rampant inequality present across the country. The top 5% of earners in the United States have seen their income rise faster than any other economic group in the country over the past four decades.

The consequences of this widening gap are unavoidable, and are especially present in Maryland. Howard County’s median household income is currently $129,549, making it the seventh-richest in the nation by family earnings. Less than a half hour away, in Baltimore city, the median household income is $54,124, according to the census bureau.

Governments seeking to rectify this inequality have a long road ahead, but the fact remains that the income rises of the past decade do not reflect all Marylanders.
